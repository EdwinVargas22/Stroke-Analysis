Matplotlib-Seaborn Analysis

Below are some graphs and analyses using the matplotlib and seaborn packages.

This bar chart tells us people who have hypertension have a higher chance of getting a stroke compared to people who don’t have hypertension. The stroke column is set as 0 for no stroke and 1 for stroke which is why the values in the y-axis are only decimals. Interesting part is, males who don’t have hypertension have a small higher chance of getting a stroke compared to females who don’t have hypertension. It reverses for the genders for people who have hypertension but the difference between females and males is a bit higher.

For people who don’t have heart diseases it is pretty equal for both genders for having a stroke but the chance is low because it is closer to 0. People who do have heart diseases have a higher chance of having a stroke compared to the people who don’t have heart diseases. Males is slightly higher than females.

It is interesting to see males led in everything single status except for “Never Smoked.” The “Formerly Smoked” status led the pack of having a higher chance of getting a stroke for both genders. We would have thought people who “Smoked” would have led the pack but this is not the case.

The “Self-Employed” status leads the pack for both genders of having a higher chance of getting a stroke. In this chart, other factors were not taken into consideration which doesn’t give the big picture of why “Self-Employed” has a higher chance of getting a stroke. It doesn’t show anything for the “Never Worked” status because most people in this dataset worked.

There really isn’t any significance on where you live that would cause you to have a higher chance of having a stroke. The one interesting part is, males are a bit higher than females for both residence types.

Very interesting to see people who are married have a higher chance of having a stroke compared to people who aren’t married. It is not saying you shouldn’t get married so you have a less chance of getting a stroke. People who usually get married are older than people who aren’t married which is probably one of the reasons why married people is way higher than not married people.

The boxplot shows there is a strong correlation between age and stroke. The older you get, the higher chance of you getting a stroke.

This shows average glucose levels has a strong correlation with stroke. The boxplot for stroke is longer than for no stroke. There are a few outliers in the no stroke boxplots for both genders.

There isn’t any indication of a strong correlation between stroke and body mass index. The boxplots line up very closely for both genders, no stroke, and stroke.

The correlations heatmap shows none of the factors have a strong correlation but it does show there is a small correlation for age with stroke, hypertension, heart disease, and average glucose levels. This tells us individually there isn’t a strong correlations but all added together it will create a strong correlation.
